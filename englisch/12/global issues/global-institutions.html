01 — The Basics

NGOs, IGOs & the alphabet
of global change

When a crisis crosses borders, no single government can solve it alone. Enter a complex web of organisations — some created by governments, others by citizens — each trying to make the world work a little better. But what exactly are they, and what separates one from another?

Type 1
NGO
Non-Governmental Organisation

NGOs are independent from governments — funded by donations, grants, or their own revenues. They range from tiny local charities to massive global operations. Some focus on emergency aid; others on long-term advocacy, research, or service delivery. What unites them: they operate outside the formal state system and usually have a mission-driven ethos.

Amnesty International Médecins Sans Frontières WWF Oxfam
Type 2
IGO
Intergovernmental Organisation

IGOs are created by treaties between sovereign states. Member governments fund and govern them together. This gives IGOs enormous formal authority and resources — but also makes them vulnerable to political deadlock and the competing interests of their members. They set international standards, coordinate global policy, and sometimes deploy peacekeepers or aid.

United Nations UNESCO World Bank WTO
💡 Key insight to investigate: Neither NGOs nor IGOs are automatically "good." Both face criticism for inefficiency, bias, lack of accountability, or even causing harm. Your job as a researcher is to look beyond the mission statement.
02 — Why It Matters

Why study these
organisations at all?

1
Power beyond borders

International organisations shape laws, trade rules, and humanitarian standards that affect billions of people who never voted for them. Understanding who holds this power — and how — is essential for any informed citizen.

2
Accountability gaps

Unlike elected governments, many global institutions are not democratically accountable. Who watches the watchmen? Studying these organisations reveals crucial questions about legitimacy and reform.

3
Real-world impact

These aren't abstract concepts. The World Bank's conditions have restructured entire economies. The UNHCR determines refugee policy. UNESCO shapes what counts as world heritage. The stakes are enormous.

03 — The Players

Key organisations
& research questions

Six carefully chosen starting points below — each with a brief introduction and curated research questions designed to push you beyond description towards genuine analysis. Pick one that interests you and go deep.

Core selection
NGO Est. 1961
Amnesty International
Human Rights · Advocacy · Research

Founded on the idea that ordinary people writing letters could free political prisoners, Amnesty has grown into a global force documenting human rights abuses in 150+ countries. Its annual reports are used by courts, journalists, and governments worldwide.

Research Questions

To what extent can NGO "naming and shaming" campaigns change the behaviour of authoritarian governments?
How does Amnesty balance the tension between universal human rights norms and cultural relativism?
Amnesty has faced criticism for alleged bias in which violations it prioritises. How should an organisation like this manage accusations of political partiality?
To what degree is Amnesty's effectiveness dependent on the political will of democratic governments to act on its findings?
Core selection
IGO Est. 1945
UNESCO
Education · Science · Culture · Heritage

UNESCO was founded on the conviction that lasting peace requires more than military treaties — it needs education, science, and shared culture. It runs global literacy initiatives, designates World Heritage Sites, and tracks whether children worldwide can access quality schooling. But can education policy really reduce inequality?

Research Questions

UNESCO's "Education for All" initiative aimed to achieve universal primary schooling by 2015 — and largely missed its targets. What does this reveal about the limits of IGO-led education reform?
To what extent does expanding access to schooling alone reduce inequality, or do quality, language of instruction, and economic context matter just as much?
UNESCO's World Heritage List has been criticised for reflecting Western cultural values more than global ones. Is the concept of "universal heritage" inherently biased?
The USA withdrew from UNESCO twice — in 1984 and 2017 — over political disagreements. What does this reveal about the fragility of IGOs that depend on powerful member states for funding?
Core selection
IGO Est. 1950
UNHCR
Refugees · Displacement · International Law

The UN Refugee Agency was created after WWII to protect displaced Europeans. Today it serves over 100 million forcibly displaced people worldwide. Operating in some of the world's most dangerous environments, the UNHCR must navigate state politics, funding crises, and the limits of international law.

Research Questions

The 1951 Refugee Convention was written in a Cold War context. To what extent is it still fit for purpose in an era of climate displacement and economic migration?
How does the UNHCR balance its mandate to protect refugees with its operational dependency on the political goodwill of host governments?
Evaluate the tension between the UNHCR's ideal of durable solutions (return, integration, resettlement) and the reality that most refugees spend decades in camps.
Should "climate refugees" receive the same legal protections as those fleeing persecution? What are the arguments for and against expanding the Refugee Convention's definition?
Core selection
NGO Est. 1971
Médecins Sans Frontières
Medical Aid · Crisis Zones · Témoignage

MSF (Doctors Without Borders) delivers emergency medical care in conflict zones and epidemic outbreaks, guided by the principle of témoignage — bearing witness and speaking out. Unlike many aid organisations, MSF frequently criticises governments and even its own donors publicly. This independence is both its strength and its political risk.

Research Questions

Does humanitarian aid prolong conflicts by sustaining populations that combatants would otherwise use as leverage? How should organisations like MSF respond to this critique?
MSF pioneered public "witnessing" — speaking out against abuses at political risk to its operations. Evaluate the argument that silence is complicity versus the case for quiet neutrality.
The 2015 bombing of MSF's Kunduz hospital by a US airstrike killed 42 people. What does this reveal about the vulnerability of humanitarian organisations in modern warfare?
To what extent does MSF's model of expatriate-led medical teams challenge, rather than build, local healthcare capacity in the countries where it operates?
Core selection
IGO Est. 1944
World Bank
Development Finance · Poverty Reduction · Loans

Created at Bretton Woods to rebuild post-war Europe, the World Bank now focuses on reducing global poverty through loans, grants, and policy advice to developing nations. It commands over $100 billion in annual commitments — but its history with "structural adjustment" programmes has left a deeply contested legacy.

Research Questions

Structural adjustment programmes conditioned World Bank loans on privatisation, deregulation, and austerity. Evaluate the evidence on whether these policies reduced or deepened poverty in recipient countries.
To what extent does the World Bank's governance structure — where voting power reflects financial contribution — undermine its stated mission of representing the world's poorest?
How should the World Bank balance the pursuit of economic growth with social and environmental safeguards when financing large infrastructure projects?
The rise of China's Belt and Road Initiative offers developing countries an alternative source of development finance. How has this changed the World Bank's influence and approach?
Core selection
IGO Est. 1945
UN Security Council
Peace & Security · Peacekeeping · Veto Power

The UNSC is the only body with legal authority to authorise international military force. Five permanent members — the US, UK, France, Russia, and China — each hold veto power, a legacy of the post-WWII world order. This structure has preserved great-power peace in some ways while enabling paralysis in others.

Research Questions

The P5 veto has been used hundreds of times, often to protect allies from accountability. Is the veto a necessary safeguard against great power conflict, or a tool for impunity?
The UNSC's permanent membership reflects 1945 power structures. Should it be reformed to include India, Brazil, or African nations? What obstacles stand in the way?
Evaluate the "Responsibility to Protect" (R2P) doctrine: does the international community have an obligation to intervene in atrocities even without state consent?
Russia's invasion of Ukraine in 2022 put a P5 member in direct violation of the UN Charter. What does this reveal about the UNSC's fundamental structural contradiction?

NGO Est. 1971
Greenpeace
Environment · Direct Action · Campaigning

Greenpeace pioneered confrontational environmental activism — sailing ships into nuclear test zones, blockading whaling vessels, scaling oil platforms. Its model relies on dramatic direct action to generate media coverage and public pressure. But does spectacle translate into lasting policy change?

Research Questions

Is confrontational direct action an effective long-term strategy for environmental policy change, or does it alienate potential allies?
Greenpeace has been criticised for opposing nuclear power despite its low carbon footprint. How should environmental NGOs navigate complex trade-offs between different ecological values?
Compare Greenpeace's approach to that of more "insider" NGOs like the WWF. What are the advantages and limitations of each model?
To what extent has the rise of youth climate movements like Fridays for Future made Greenpeace's model of protest less distinctive or necessary?
IGO Est. 1948
World Health Organization
Global Health · Standards · Emergency Response

The WHO sets international health standards, coordinates pandemic responses, and runs vaccination programmes. Its work reaches every country — but the Covid-19 crisis exposed deep questions about its speed, independence, and relationship with powerful member states.

Research Questions

Did the WHO's handling of the early Covid-19 outbreak reveal structural weaknesses in how IGOs depend on member state cooperation?
To what extent does the WHO's funding model — where wealthy donor states and private foundations hold disproportionate influence — compromise its independence?
How should the WHO navigate tensions between national sovereignty (e.g. data-sharing refusal) and global health security?
Evaluate what the WHO's eradication of smallpox reveals about the conditions needed for genuine global health success.
IGO Est. 2002
International Criminal Court
War Crimes · Accountability · International Law

The ICC prosecutes individuals for genocide, war crimes, and crimes against humanity when national courts cannot or will not act. It represents a historic attempt to hold even heads of state accountable — yet the USA, China, Russia, and India are not members, raising sharp questions about universality and justice.

Research Questions

Critics argue the ICC disproportionately prosecutes African leaders while ignoring crimes by Western powers and their allies. Is this a fair critique?
Can international criminal justice deliver genuine deterrence, or does it primarily serve a symbolic function of establishing historical record?
The ICC's indictment of sitting heads of state often prevents peace negotiations. How should the court balance justice with the pragmatic demands of conflict resolution?
Without its own enforcement mechanism, the ICC depends entirely on state cooperation to arrest suspects. Does this undermine the very idea of international criminal law?
IGO Est. 1988
IPCC
Climate Science · Policy Assessment · Reports

The IPCC synthesises the work of thousands of scientists into Assessment Reports that form the scientific basis for global climate negotiations. Its reports are consensus documents, approved line by line by governments — which creates both credibility and limitations.

Research Questions

Does the IPCC's requirement for government-approved consensus systematically understate the severity of climate risks?
How should science communicate uncertainty without being misrepresented by those who wish to cast doubt on its findings?
IPCC reports describe what will happen; they do not prescribe what to do. Does separating "facts" from "values" in this way serve — or undermine — effective climate action?
Given that IPCC findings have been broadly accepted since 1990, why has global CO₂ output continued to rise? What does this tell us about the limits of science-to-policy translation?
NGO Est. 1942
Oxfam
Poverty · Inequality · Advocacy

Oxfam began by sending food to occupied Greece. Today it runs development programmes in 70+ countries and publishes influential research on global inequality — famously reporting that the world's 8 richest people hold as much wealth as the poorest 50%. But its data and its conduct have both come under scrutiny.

Research Questions

Oxfam's wealth-inequality statistics are widely shared but also contested by economists. What are the methodological challenges of measuring global inequality — and why does how we define it matter?
A 2018 sexual exploitation scandal in Haiti severely damaged Oxfam's reputation. What does this reveal about power dynamics and safeguarding failures in international aid?
Oxfam argues that inequality itself — not just poverty — is the core problem. Evaluate this claim: does reducing inequality necessarily improve the wellbeing of the poorest?
Is there a contradiction in a wealthy charity organisation lobbying against the very tax structures that allow wealthy donors to fund it?
NGO Est. 1978
Human Rights Watch
Documentation · Advocacy · Legal Research

HRW produces meticulous, on-the-ground reports documenting abuses from North Korea to the US prison system — cross-referencing satellite imagery, survivor interviews, and legal documents. Its investigative rigour gives its findings weight in courts and parliaments. But rigour takes time, and emergencies don't wait.

Research Questions

How does HRW's evidence-based documentation approach compare to Amnesty International's campaign-driven model? What are the respective strengths and weaknesses?
HRW reports on authoritarian states are often dismissed as "foreign interference." How should international human rights monitoring respond to charges of sovereignty violation?
HRW receives significant funding from Western philanthropists. To what extent does this funding geography shape which rights violations receive the most attention?
In an era of social media and viral journalism, does HRW's slow, evidence-heavy model still have a comparative advantage — or is it being outpaced by citizen journalism?
04 — The Big Issues

Global challenges these
organisations are confronting

Climate Change

Reducing emissions, managing the transition to renewables, and addressing loss and damage in the most vulnerable nations — while balancing development needs.

Education & Equality

Whether schooling can genuinely break cycles of poverty — and who decides what counts as quality education across vastly different cultural and economic contexts.

Poverty & Inequality

Not just lack of income, but structural exclusion from power, healthcare, education, and justice — and the widening gap between global winners and losers.

Human Rights

Freedom from torture, arbitrary detention, and discrimination — and the harder questions of who defines "universal" rights in a world of vastly different legal and cultural traditions.

Peace & Conflict

Preventing wars, protecting civilians in active conflict, demobilising child soldiers, and rebuilding societies after violence — while navigating the competing interests of powerful states.

Displacement & Migration

Over 100 million people are displaced — by war, climate, persecution, and poverty. Who has the right to move, who is protected, and who bears responsibility for those on the move?

05 — Your Turn

Now do your own research

The organisations above are starting points, not destinations. The best research goes deeper — primary sources, critical perspectives, and your own carefully reasoned conclusions. Here's how to approach it.

→
Start with the mission

Read the organisation's own website, annual reports, and stated goals. What do they claim to do, and for whom?

→
Find the critics

Who disagrees? Journalists, academics, governments, and affected communities often have powerful counter-narratives. Seek them out deliberately.

→
Evaluate the evidence

What data exists about actual impact? Who collected it? What might they have incentive to overstate or omit? Form your own reasoned judgement.
