The Kingdom Unites – and 'Disunites'?

📜 How did the UK come to be?

England, Scotland, Wales and Ireland were originally independent countries. England gradually came to dominate the others. Wales was fully annexed in 1542, Scotland and England united in 1707, and in 1801 the union with Ireland created the 'Kingdom of Great Britain and Ireland'. After the Irish War of Independence (1919–1921), Ireland was divided – only Protestant Northern Ireland remained part of the UK.

⚖️ Devolution – What does it mean?

Definition

Devolution means the transfer of political powers from a central government to regional governments. In 1998, Northern Ireland, Scotland and Wales were granted their own law-making institutions in areas of regional interest. England, however, still lacks its own regional parliament.

In 2014, a referendum on Scottish independence was narrowly defeated. However, since Scotland voted Remain in the Brexit referendum while England voted Leave, the debate about Scottish independence has been re-energised.

A 'Disunited' England? The North-South Divide

✂️ What is the North-South Divide?

The North-South divide refers to significant differences within England in terms of climate, landscape, mentality and – most importantly – wealth. Traditionally, Britain has always been a class-ridden society, with the rich and poor leading very different lives. This divide has deep historical roots and continues to deepen today.

18th–19th Century: Industrialisation

The North became the heartland of industrialisation: coal mines in Lancashire, Yorkshire and Durham; heavy industry in Sheffield, Manchester and Newcastle. The Victorian-era slums were notorious for poverty, crime and poor sanitation.

Early 20th Century: Welfare State (1942)

The gap between rich and poor led the government to set up welfare policies. 1942 marked the beginning of the comprehensive welfare state – aiming at security 'from the cradle to the grave'.

1970s–80s: Deindustrialisation & Thatcher

Economic crisis hit heavy industry hard. Conservative PM Margaret Thatcher privatised nationalised industries and weakened trade unions – cementing the North-South divide. The South, especially London, shifted to finance and services.

Today: The Divide Deepens

Public investment money is still not equally distributed and investors tend to prefer the South. The 2016 Brexit vote starkly revealed the divide: many northern Leave areas felt 'left behind' by Westminster.

🏭 Key Causes of the Divide

1
Industrial Legacy

The North's economy was built on industries that collapsed (coal, steel, textiles). The South diversified earlier into services.

2
Unequal Investment

Government spending on infrastructure (especially transport) is massively skewed towards London. The city receives over 10× more per capita than the North East.

3
'Brain Drain'

Talented young people from the North move South for better-paid jobs and higher education, further depleting northern human capital.

4
Political Centralisation

The UK is one of the most centralised democracies in the developed world. Decisions that affect the North are made in Westminster by a London-centric political class.

❌ Common Myth

"People in the North should just move to where the jobs are."

Reality: Average London house prices (£535,000) make relocation impossible for most on northern wages. It also ignores social ties, family responsibilities and the value of thriving regional communities.

Empire, Commonwealth and Immigration

🌍 From Empire to Commonwealth

From the 16th century, British companies set up trading posts around the world to secure goods like tea, sugar and rubber. These often became colonies – large areas of land under British control. The exploitation of people (including the slave trade) and natural resources was an integral part of the British Empire.

By the 19th century, Britain ruled roughly one quarter of the world's population, making it the most powerful nation on earth. During the 20th century, most colonies gained independence. Today, 54 former colonies are associated through the Commonwealth, but many still struggle with the economic and political legacy of colonialism.

✈️ Immigration: Post-War to Today

After World War II, Britain faced a labour shortage. The government encouraged immigrants from Commonwealth nations – India, Pakistan, the Caribbean – to come and work. Most settled in London and industrial towns in the Midlands and North, making Britain a much more ethnically and culturally diverse country.

In the 21st century, many people from Eastern Europe (following EU enlargement) also came to work in the UK. Since Brexit, this flow has significantly reduced.

"There is an ongoing debate about the benefits and drawbacks of mass immigration to the UK. Systemic racism, discrimination, riots, and ethnic parallel societies are some aspects of a multicultural Britain. Nevertheless, there is a broad consensus that tolerance and diversity have profited the country and are now an integral part of Britishness." – Textbook (adapted), p. 61

🌐 The Commonwealth Today

  • 54 member nations – former British colonies
  • Hospitals, railways and democratic structures were retained as part of the colonial legacy
  • Many former colonies struggle with rebuilding economies and ethnic tensions caused by arbitrary colonial borders
  • English has become the world language – a positive legacy of the Empire
  • Colonialism also caused the destruction of local political systems and extensive suffering

Post-Brexit Britain: Heading for New Shores?

🗳️ The Brexit Referendum

After WWII and the disintegration of its Empire, the UK reoriented itself and joined the European Union in 1973. However, it remained sceptical – perceiving the EU Parliament as a threat to national sovereignty.

On 23 June 2016, a referendum was held on UK membership of the EU. Many Conservatives (Tories) and the populist UKIP campaigned for 'Leave'. Ultimately, Leave won by a close margin (51.89% vs. 48.11%). The UK finally left the EU on 31 January 2020.

📰 Tabloid vs. Quality Press – A Key Distinction

Quality / Broadsheet papers (Guardian, Telegraph, Times, Independent): written for an educated readership; use formal language; provide comprehensive analysis and critical insight into complex issues.

Tabloid / Popular papers (Sun, Daily Mail, Mirror): written for a mass readership; make events appear sensationalist and dramatic; complex issues are frequently simplified and presented in a biased way.

Analysing a Newspaper Article

🔍 What to look for when analysing a non-fictional text

A strong text analysis moves beyond simply summarising the content. You need to examine how the author constructs their argument and why they use specific language choices.

📐

Structure & Organisation

  • How is the text organised? Does it use a clear intro–body–conclusion structure?
  • How does the author sequence arguments?
  • Does the text start with a strong hook?
  • Is there a clear thesis / position stated?
🗣️

Tone & Register

  • Is the language formal or informal?
  • Is the tone optimistic, ironic, alarmed, triumphant?
  • Does the author use first person ("we", "I") to create solidarity?
  • Tabloid vs. quality paper language
🎭

Rhetorical Devices

  • Metaphor / imagery (e.g. ship metaphor in the Times article)
  • Alliteration, repetition, tricolon
  • Irony / understatement
  • Rhetorical questions to engage reader
  • Emotive language to appeal to fear/hope
⚖️

Arguments & Evidence

  • What is the author's main claim (thesis)?
  • What evidence / examples are used?
  • Are counterarguments acknowledged?
  • Is the evidence reliable and relevant?

🛠️ Useful Analytical Phrases

Introducing arguments: "The author argues that…" / "The article claims that…" / "According to the author…"

Analysing language: "The use of [device] creates/suggests/implies…" / "By using [word], the author conveys…"

Evaluating: "This argument is convincing because…" / "However, the author fails to consider…"

Linking ideas: "Furthermore…" / "In contrast…" / "This is reinforced by…" / "Ultimately…"

Writing a Comment / Editorial

✍️ What is a Comment?

A comment (also called an editorial or opinion piece) is a type of essay in which you give your opinion on a controversial topic. Your aim is to present a clear, well-argued position and to convince the reader. Comments in quality newspapers are written in a formal register.

1

Structure Your Comment

📌 Introduction

  • Hook: anecdote, striking fact, rhetorical question, or bold claim
  • Context: brief background on the topic
  • Thesis: your position, clearly stated in one sentence
  • Topic sentence: preview of your main arguments

📝 Main Body

  • One paragraph per argument
  • PEEL: Point → Explain → Evidence → Link back
  • Include and refute at least one counterargument
  • Use linking words: Furthermore, However, Nevertheless, Ultimately…

🎯 Conclusion

  • Summarise your position briefly
  • Do NOT introduce new arguments
  • Give an outlook: future implications or a call to action
  • Strong closing sentence that resonates
2

Argue Effectively – and Refute Counterarguments

⚔️ The Art of Argumentation

A strong comment does not just present one side. It acknowledges opposing views and then refutes them. This makes your argument more credible and shows analytical depth.

How to Refute a Counterargument

  1. Concede: "It is true that some argue…" / "Admittedly, …"
  2. Introduce your refutation: "However, …" / "Nevertheless, this ignores the fact that…"
  3. Strengthen your position: "In fact, …" / "The evidence clearly shows that…"

PEEL – Building a Paragraph

Point: State your argument clearly in one sentence.
Explain: Develop and clarify the point.
Evidence: Support with facts, statistics, or an example.
Link: Connect back to your thesis or lead into the next point.

3

Use Stylistic Devices to Elevate Your Writing

🎨 Recommended Stylistic Devices for Comments

  • Rhetorical question: "Can we really afford to ignore this issue any longer?"
  • Tricolon / Listing: "This policy is costly, counterproductive, and fundamentally unjust."
  • Metaphor / Analogy: "Brexit was not a leap of faith – it was a leap off a cliff."
  • Concessive clause: "While it is true that…, the evidence suggests that…"
  • Direct address: "Consider for a moment what this means for our society."
  • Statistics / data: concrete numbers make arguments more credible

Register reminder: A quality newspaper comment uses formal vocabulary, complex sentences, and an objective but engaged tone. Avoid slang, contractions, and overly emotional language.

4

How to Write a Strong Introduction

🚀 Four Proven Introduction Strategies

A
Striking Statistic

"Over 10 million people in the UK currently live in poverty. Yet the government's flagship 'Levelling Up' fund amounts to less than £50 per affected person per year."

B
Personal Anecdote / Vivid Scene

"On the morning of 24 June 2016, Britain woke up to a country it barely recognised." (→ Matt Chorley, The Times)

C
Rhetorical Question

"Is it truly democratic when a nation votes to leave an institution, only to find that the promised benefits never materialise?"

D
Provocative Thesis

"The Brexit experiment has not failed – it was never given a fair chance to succeed."

📚 Education & Society

✅ Comment Writing Checklist

  • My introduction hooks the reader and states my thesis clearly
  • Each body paragraph follows the PEEL structure
  • I use at least three different stylistic devices (named and intentional)
  • I acknowledge and refute at least one counterargument
  • I use formal vocabulary and sentence structures throughout
  • My conclusion summarises my position and gives an outlook
  • I have checked spelling, grammar and punctuation
  • My comment is between 250 and 350 words

⚠️ Common Mistakes to Avoid

  • Starting every sentence with "I" → vary sentence structures
  • Stating opinions without evidence → always support claims with facts
  • Ignoring counterarguments → refute them to show analytical depth
  • Using informal language → "lots of", "kids", "stuff" are not appropriate for a quality paper
  • A weak conclusion → don't just repeat the introduction; give a meaningful outlook
  • Forgetting stylistic devices → plan them in advance, don't add them as an afterthought

Summary & Key Takeaways

✅ What you should know

After completing this unit, you should be able to discuss all of the following with confidence and evidence.

1
The UK is a union of four nations

England, Scotland, Wales and Northern Ireland – each with distinct identities, histories and political arrangements. Devolution has given Scotland, Wales and Northern Ireland their own assemblies; England remains centralised.

2
The North-South divide is real and measurable

Decades of deindustrialisation, unequal investment and political centralisation have created persistent gaps in income, health, education and infrastructure between northern and southern England.

3
Empire and immigration have shaped modern Britain

Britain's colonial past brought enormous wealth but caused immense suffering. Post-war immigration from Commonwealth countries made Britain the diverse, multicultural society it is today.

4
Brexit revealed and deepened existing divisions

The 2016 referendum exposed fault lines between North and South, young and old, urban and rural. Britain left the EU in 2020 and is still redefining its global role.

5
Media shapes how we understand political events

Tabloids and quality papers frame issues very differently. Critical readers must distinguish between fact and opinion, and be alert to tone, bias and rhetorical manipulation.

6
A good comment has clear structure and strong argumentation

Introduction with thesis → PEEL paragraphs → counterargument refutation → forward-looking conclusion. Use formal register and stylistic devices intentionally.

🎯 Essential Facts to Remember

  • Devolution: Scotland, Wales, Northern Ireland received assemblies in 1998/1999
  • Brexit referendum: 23 June 2016 – Leave won 51.89%
  • UK left the EU: 31 January 2020
  • North-South salary gap: approximately £6,000 per year
  • Transport investment: London receives over 10× more per capita than the North East
  • Life expectancy gap: up to 2.6 years between regions
  • Commonwealth: 54 member nations – former British colonies
  • Scottish independence referendum: 2014 – narrowly defeated
