Step 2

What Does "Narrative" Mean Here?

🎯 The Core Idea

Media doesn't just record what happens. It builds events into a story — and every story needs choices about what goes in, what gets left out, and who looks like the hero.

📚 Four Things Every Story-Builder Does

Selection: deciding what to show at all. Out of everything that happened, only some of it makes it into the report, the broadcast, the post.

Emphasis: deciding what to make the centre of attention. The same event can be led with a different detail, a different image, a different headline.

Framing: deciding who looks like the hero, the villain, or the victim. The facts can stay identical while the framing changes the whole impression.

Omission: deciding what simply doesn't get mentioned. What's left out is often just as powerful as what's put in.

From Raw Events to a Story

Think of it as a funnel. The same raw material can come out as a very different story, depending on which choices get made along the way.

Raw events
→
select emphasise frame omit
→
A story

Usually, you never see this happening — you just get the finished story. That's what makes the next part of this page unusual.

Step 4

Seeing It in the Book

🎯 Why The Hunger Games Makes This Visible

In most media, the four choices on the last page happen behind the scenes — an editor in an office, an algorithm you never see. The Hunger Games puts that process on screen, inside the story itself, as actual characters doing an actual job.

🎮 The Gamemakers as Editors

The Gamemakers don't just referee the arena — they actively reshape it to keep the broadcast compelling: setting fires to drive tributes together, releasing mutated creatures to manufacture danger, engineering the pace of events like a showrunner edits an episode. They are, quite literally, deciding what story the audience gets.

📹 The Broadcast Feed Is Curated, Not Raw

Cameras don't follow everyone equally. They follow whoever is currently interesting — whoever is providing drama, romance, or conflict. Viewers at home never see "the arena as it is." They see a curated story built from selected footage. The same arena could be edited into a completely different story depending on whose feed gets airtime.

🎁 Narrative Has Stakes, Not Just Style

Sponsor gifts are tied directly to which story a tribute is performing. The "star-crossed lovers" framing isn't just charming — it's the reason Peeta receives medicine he might not have gotten otherwise. In this world, controlling the narrative is a matter of survival, not just popularity.

🕊️ Rue's Death: Katniss Turns the Cameras Around

Look at what Katniss does after Rue dies. She doesn't just mourn — she arranges the scene: the song, the flowers, the salute. She knows the cameras will have to broadcast it, and she builds a picture that says this was a child, and you did this. For a moment, a tribute is writing the story instead of the Gamemakers — using their own broadcast against them.

And the bread from District 11 is the audience answering. A district publicly honouring another district's tribute has never happened before — a single moment, framed the right way, is starting to carry meaning the people in power never intended.

📣 The Fifth Move: Changing the Event Itself

Then, at the very end of the chapter, the announcement: from now on, two tributes can win — if they come from the same district. Read that as a Gamemaker decision, not just a plot twist. The "star-crossed lovers" story is the broadcast's biggest asset, and until this moment it could only end one way. Rue's death has also just handed the audience a dangerous new story to care about — the Gamemakers urgently need a better one.

Notice what kind of move this is. Selection, emphasis, framing and omission all shape how events are shown. This is something more drastic: intervention — changing what can actually happen, so that the story you want becomes possible. The Gamemakers aren't editing the footage anymore. They're rewriting reality inside the arena to serve the broadcast.

Step 6 · Main Task

The Rule Change: Same Arena, New Story

🎯 Your Task

At the very end of chapter 18, the Gamemakers announce a rule change: two victors are now possible — if they come from the same district. Your job is to analyse that announcement as a piece of media strategy, using today's lens. One announcement, a few seconds of airtime — and both the presentation of the Games and their possible outcomes are different. Write your analysis (in English) using the five prompts below; you'll submit it in the form on the next page.

✏️ What to Write

  1. Before: Just before the announcement, how could the Games end? Spell out the possible endings — and what each of them would mean for the "star-crossed lovers" story.
  2. After: What new ending has just become possible? Who is the rule obviously written for — and how can you tell from the way it's worded ("from the same district")?
  3. Why now? Rue's death, the flowers, District 11's gift — all of that just went out on the broadcast. Why might the Gamemakers want to hand the audience a new story at exactly this moment?
  4. Presentation and outcome: This move goes further than framing — it changes what can actually happen, not just how it's shown. What does that tell you about what the Games really are: a competition, or a show?
  5. Transfer (short): Name one real example where the people running something changed its rules or format at least partly for the audience — reality TV, a sports competition, televised debates… One or two sentences are enough.

✅ What a Good Analysis Looks Like

  • Your "before" and "after" are concrete — actual possible endings, not just "it changes things."
  • You connect the timing of the announcement to what happens earlier in the chapter (Rue, the flowers, the bread).
  • You treat the rule change as a decision made by people with goals — and you say what those goals are.
  • Your real-world example is specific (a named show, sport or format), not "TV in general."

If you're stuck on "before": before the announcement, there was exactly one way the romance story could end — spell it out, and think about how an audience that loves the couple would feel watching it.

If you're stuck on "why now": think like a broadcaster. Your most popular storyline is doomed by the rules, and your audience has just seen something that made them angry at the show itself. What do you do?

One more thing to check: Katniss and Peeta aren't the only pair the rule applies to — Cato and Clove from District 2 are also both alive. Does that make the rule neutral, or does it just make the favouritism deniable?

Last Step

Wrapping Up

🌟 Key Insight to Take With You

The Gamemakers didn't just tell the story differently — they changed the rules of the world so that a better story became possible. When the same people control both the event and the broadcast, "presentation" stops being cosmetic: it decides what can happen. And yet the useful question stays the same: "whose story is this, and what is it built to do?" Asking that question is the actual skill here, not blanket suspicion.
