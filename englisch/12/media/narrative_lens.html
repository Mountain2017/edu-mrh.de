How Facts Become Stories

Media turns facts into stories.

Your teacher isn't in class today, so this page runs the lesson. Work through it top to bottom — it's built to take about as long as a normal lesson, not longer.

Step 2

What Does "Narrative" Mean Here?

🎯 The Core Idea

Media doesn't just record what happens. It builds events into a story — and every story needs choices about what goes in, what gets left out, and who looks like the hero.

📚 Four Things Every Story-Builder Does

Selection: deciding what to show at all. Out of everything that happened, only some of it makes it into the report, the broadcast, the post.

Emphasis: deciding what to make the centre of attention. The same event can be led with a different detail, a different image, a different headline.

Framing: deciding who looks like the hero, the villain, or the victim. The facts can stay identical while the framing changes the whole impression.

Omission: deciding what simply doesn't get mentioned. What's left out is often just as powerful as what's put in.

From Raw Events to a Story

Think of it as a funnel. The same raw material can come out as a very different story, depending on which choices get made along the way.

Raw events
→
select emphasise frame omit
→
A story

Usually, you never see this happening — you just get the finished story. That's what makes the next part of this page unusual.

Step 3

Seeing It in the Book

🎯 Why The Hunger Games Makes This Visible

In most media, the four choices on the last page happen behind the scenes — an editor in an office, an algorithm you never see. The Hunger Games puts that process on screen, inside the story itself, as actual characters doing an actual job.

🎮 The Gamemakers as Editors

The Gamemakers don't just referee the arena — they actively reshape it to keep the broadcast compelling: setting fires to drive tributes together, releasing mutated creatures to manufacture danger, engineering the pace of events like a showrunner edits an episode. They are, quite literally, deciding what story the audience gets.

📹 The Broadcast Feed Is Curated, Not Raw

Cameras don't follow everyone equally. They follow whoever is currently interesting — whoever is providing drama, romance, or conflict. Viewers at home never see "the arena as it is." They see a curated story built from selected footage. The same arena could be edited into a completely different story depending on whose feed gets airtime.

🎁 Narrative Has Stakes, Not Just Style

Sponsor gifts are tied directly to which story a tribute is performing. The "star-crossed lovers" framing isn't just charming — it's the reason Peeta receives medicine he might not have gotten otherwise. In this world, controlling the narrative is a matter of survival, not just popularity.

🕊️ When a Moment Becomes a Symbol

Rue's death, and the way District 11 reacts to it, shows the flip side: a single moment can be picked up, framed, and carried far beyond the original event — turning into something the people in power never intended and can't fully control.

Step 5

Not Just a Fictional Games

💡 The Same Thing Happens Outside the Book

Selection, emphasis, framing, and omission aren't unique to a fictional arena. They happen to every real event you've ever heard about — on the news, on social media, anywhere a story gets told about something that happened. The next task asks you to go find it for yourself.

Step 6 · Main Task

Framing Comparison

🎯 Your Task

Pick one real event from the last few weeks — something with actual news coverage, not something obscure. Then find how two different sources covered it, and compare how each one told the story.

Choosing Your Event

Good choices are events that got covered by more than one type of outlet — a political decision, a protest, a court case, a strike, an election result, a major sports controversy. Avoid events so narrow that only one source covered them at all.

🗞️ A Reference, Not a Requirement

You don't have to use these specific outlets — pick any two real sources covering your event. This is just to help you think about what kinds of differences to expect.

Often sober / measured

Wire services & broadsheets

Tend toward neutral language, more context, less emotional framing. Still make selection and emphasis choices — just less obviously.

Often charged / dramatic

Tabloids & opinion-heavy outlets

Tend toward strong headlines, clear heroes/villains, emotional language. Easier to spot framing in these.

Often human-interest led

Broadcast / human-interest coverage

Tends to lead with a person's story rather than the policy or numbers — emphasis shifted onto an individual.

Often data-led

Data journalism & explainers

Tends to foreground numbers, charts, timelines — selection here is about which statistics get shown.

✏️ What to Compare

  1. Headlines: Write down both headlines exactly. What's different?
  2. Emphasis: What does each source lead with — what's the very first thing you're told?
  3. Framing: If there's a person or group involved, who comes across as the hero, villain, or victim in each version — if anyone?
  4. Omission: Is there something one source mentions that the other leaves out entirely?
  5. Why might they differ? One sentence — think about audience, ownership, or the outlet's usual angle.

✅ What a Good Comparison Looks Like

  • You picked one specific real event with available coverage from more than one source.
  • You quote or closely paraphrase what each source actually said — not what you assume they'd say.
  • You name a real difference, not just "one was nicer than the other."
  • Your answer to "why might they differ" goes beyond "because they're biased" — it names something specific about the source.

If you're stuck on framing: look at the verbs. "Protesters clashed with police" and "police cracked down on protesters" describe the same event but assign action differently.

If you're stuck on omission: check whether one article includes a number, a quote, or a piece of context that the other simply never mentions.

If your two sources look identical: that's a real finding too — but check the headline and the opening line again before concluding that.

Last Step

Wrapping Up

🌟 Key Insight to Take With You

Noticing that every story is built doesn't mean every story is a lie. The useful question isn't "can I trust media?" — it's "whose story is this, and what is it built to do?" Asking that question is the actual skill here, not blanket suspicion.
